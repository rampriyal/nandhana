STEM BORER AND STRIGA - PARTIAL ROOT PARASITE IN SORGHUM

Sorghum is an important cereal crop grown worldwide, but it faces challenges from pests like stem borers and parasitic weeds like Striga.

Stem Borer in Sorghum

Stem borers are major pests in sorghum crops. They bore into the stems of the plant, causing significant yield losses.

Life Cycle of Stem Borer

  • Egg stage
  • Larva stage (when most damage occurs)- 28 to 50 days with 7 instars
  • Pupa stage (Inside the stem)- 7 to 10 days
  • Adult moth stage

Control Measures for Stem Borer

  • Biological control using parasitoids like Trichogramma chilonis (egg parasitoids) minutum, Bracon chinensis and Apanteles flavipes, (larval parasitoids)
  • Chemical control with insecticides like Karate 5EC and Diazinon 10G
  • Intercropping with lablab in 4:1 ratio and resistant varieties like ICSV 700

Interaction Between Volatiles Released by Sorghum and Stem Borer

  • When stem borer larvae feed on sorghum, the plant releases volatiles like green leaf volatiles (GLVs), terpenes (e.g., linalool, (E)-β-farnesene), and jasmonic acid-related compounds
  • Some of the volatiles emitted by sorghum can attract stem borer adults for oviposition (egg-laying).

    • Striga - The Partial Root Parasite

    Striga, commonly known as witchweed, is a parasitic plant that attacks the roots of sorghum, reducing yields significantly.

    Striga's Impact on Sorghum

    • Reduces nutrient and water absorption by the sorghum plant
    • Causes stunted growth and lower yields
    • Highly resilient and difficult to control

    Control Measures for Striga

    • Use of resistant sorghum varieties like SRN39, IS9830, Framida, 555, Dobbs, Serena and N13
    • Trap crops like cotton, sunflower and linseed that stimulate Striga germination but do not support its growth
    • Herbicide application eg:Imazapyr and Pyrithiobac.

    Interaction between striga and sorghum volatiles

  • Strigolactones are plant hormones that promote mycorrhizal fungi interactions in healthy plants but also serve as germination stimulants for Striga seeds.
  • Certain root-associated beneficial microorganisms (e.g., bacteria and fungi) can help the host plant defend against Striga by degrading strigolactones or inhibiting Striga attachment.
  • Some non-host plants, like Desmodium, used in a “push-pull” system release volatiles that either inhibit Striga germination or stimulate premature germination when Striga seeds are far from host roots, thereby starving the parasite.
  • When Striga attaches, sorghum may release volatile organic compounds (VOCs) as part of an induced defense mechanism.
